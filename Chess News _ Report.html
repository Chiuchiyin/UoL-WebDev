Introduction

Purpose

The goal of this website is to provide coverage of trending chess events and tournaments in an engaging fashion. The target demographic ranges from chess enthusiasts to novice players and experienced players alike. It caters to novice players by providing statistics (in the 'About Us' page) regarding the popularity and international appeal of chess as a means of bandwagon. It also caters to more experienced players by referencing advanced chess openings (i.e. mentioning of opening repertoire such as the Petroff opening in the 'Current News' page) and international tournament results at the highest level of play. The website aims to inspire the masses to pick up the sport, and to educate and inform chess enthusiasts on recent developments in the chess world.

Structure

The ‘Home’ and ‘Current News’ pages employ compact grid layouts to present dense information in the form of a newspaper. The layouts are uniform yet not identical; these pages have a similar overall structure to visually group articles and increase cohesion, but have distinct layouts which engage the reader to probe each section further.

The 'Home' page has a carousel that compacts many CTAs into a small space, thereby reducing vertical scrolling and maintaining accessibility. The 'Current News' page uses a sidebar as an aside to put tangential information and removes clutter from the main content. The ‘Top Players’ page is comfortable yet dynamic, as the negative space considerations around the player column cards and animated timeline contrast the dense, compact layouts of the grid layout pages. As a result, the content is rendered scannable and yields improvements in legibility and comprehension.

A unified structure exists between the column cards, which are separated into distinct sections by a decorative element. This decorative element serves as a section divider throughout the web site, creating a thematic feature that creates a stable theme. The page structure for the ‘Contact Us’ and ‘About Us’ pages mirror the comfortable feel of the ‘Top Players’ page, which was intended to provide the reader with relief following the information overload of the grid layouts presented in the ‘Home’ and ‘Current News’ pages.

The comfortable layouts contain less textual information and rely on visual imagery to convey a less dense yet appealing message. For instance, the ‘Contact Us’ page uses hypermedia (including social media icons and Scalable Vector Graphics) and interactivity (an embedded Google Maps and a contact form) to transmit its message, as well as a main banner that occupies the majority of the viewport and primes the user for a more visually-driven layout. Similarly, the uniquely identifying structure of the ‘About Us’ page is a Gutenberg Diagram-inspired layout, which prepares the user for the primarily visual grid layout containing icons and statistics. The grid layout creates a visually striking impression by contrasting white and dark themed chess background.

Inspiration

FIDE World Chess Championship 2021

The recent FIDE World Chess Championship was a fourteen-game match between the reigning champion, Magnus Carlsen, and formidable challenger, Ian Nepomnichtchi. The matches generally take place under classical time controls, which result in slower, more analytical chess games. Despite the relaxed nature of the games, I often found myself waking up early to witness the matches and accompanying commentary. Guided by the Stockfish engine, the detailed analysis conducted by higher-level players revealed the hidden dynamics of each position and ultimately provided more insight into the game.

As an avid chess player myself, designing this chess-themed website inspired me to search for more opportunities to play the game. Having realized that this event must have captured the intrigue of many novice players, I could only imagine the extent to which the commentators’ input would benefit a seasoned player. I thus aspired to mirror this experience in an inclusive and user-friendly interface that highlighted key chess events on an international scale and catered to players of all backgrounds and levels. In addition, the immense success of the Netflix series, 'The Queen's Gambit' – the platform’s most watched show in sixty-three countries – resulted in a substantial increase in the number of novice chess players, thereby indicating a large consumer base with high demand.

Chess.com's Responsive Design

Over the course of this project, I have since become much more observant and sensitive to website designs that I encounter. For instance, I experimented with the screen dimensions to estimate the media queries used. I also carefully inspected the fluidity of layout arrangements, as flexible images and text were sized in relative units (i.e. ems in relatin to the parent element or rem in relation to the root element) in order to adapt to different screen dimensions, reinforce design fidelity, and maximize accessibility. Avoiding fixed size units (i.e. px) has the added benefit of supporting browser zoom and font size adjustments based on a user's changes to the default browser font size.

I initially struggled to adapt the sidebar for smaller screen sizes on the ‘Current News’ page. However, I soon realized that relocating the sidebar would reduce clutter and enhance the user interface. Otherwise, elements could be rendered invisible on tablet and mobile devices, as may be seen with the carousel on the ‘Home’ page, the timeline images in the ‘Top Players’ page, and the image text overlay on the ‘Home’ and ‘Current News’ pages. In this way, a custom-tailored user experience was created for different devices by eliminating horizontal scrolling, ensuring the visibility of CTAs, scaling the font for readability, and eliminating bandwidth-intensive elements that would increase load times. After some trial and error, I ultimately decided to streamline the layout arrangement process by adopting a mobile-first approach. This enabled me to identify the critical content in the hierarchy of information and incrementally support large-screen devices from a simple initial framework.

WCAG Accessibility Standards

Throughout this project, the interface was designed with WCAG accessibility standards in mind and a focus on luminance contrast ratios, in order to render the site more widely accessible to both general users and those with disabilities. The implementation of a contrast toggling button thus took colour contrast compliance into account, with a low contrast mode that caters to dyslexic individuals. In light of the consequences associated with excessive animation on users with vestibular disorders, I decided to reserve animated content for essential purposes, such as demarcating important interactive elements and ensuring smoother transitions for elements floating into the viewport. As such, I employed animations that were nuanced with ease-in and ease-out considerations at appropriate, nuanced intervals.

Moreover, the website headers were strategically sized and nested according to relative importance in order to guide assistive technologies, maintain a logical flow of information, and ultimately present a clear visual hierarchy. For instance, the use of “Aria-label” allows various elements to be catalogued by screen readers, which facilitates navigation, provides a clear frame of reference for user interface controls, and enhances the identification and classification of key parts of code. As a result, the visual hierarchy ensures a highly-organized information architecture through a consistent layout and the appropriate use of semantic elements.

Accessibility

Accessibility dropdown menu which offers font legibility, link underlining, and grayscale mode

The accessibility dropdown menu is indicated by a univerisally recognized wheelchair symbol, which immediately establishes its purpose. The first accessibility option uses an objectively legible font, Arial, in place of all font families on the web page. Designed specifically to enhance online readability, sans-serif fonts are considered more legible than decorative, narrow, and serif fonts. The second accessibility option underlines links on the page to emphasize their importance and demarcate the links as CTAs. Underlining is traditionally used to indicate a link, which not only ensures that users will not misinterpret its meaning or intent, but also distinguishes between interactive and purely visual elements. The third accessibility option offers a grayscale mode, which removes bright and distracting saturated colours for general users and improves readability for colour-blind users. This further reduces eye strain under low light conditions, especially for dense blocks of text. Each accessibility option is accompanied by a corresponding high-contrast tooltip that clearly indicates the accessibility feature. This user-triggered GUI element becomes visible when a mouse-hover gesture is detected and provides a brief, temporary description that avoids collisions with critical main content elements.

Contrast toggle button for legibility and back-to-top button to reduce vertical scrolling

The default colour scheme is the high luminance contrast mode, which is ideal to reduce eye strain for users with visual impairments, such as older users with degraded colour contrast sensitivity. On the contrary, a low luminance contrast ratio, such as gray text on a dark-gray background, impairs legibility and reduces the content’s findability and perceived importance. Low contrast text is similar to fine print in the sense that it gives the impression of an attempt to obscure information or deceive the user. A user experiencing difficulty locating content would diminish the overall user experience, especially on smaller screens or under the glare of the sun.

Gray or dimmed features are also conventionally associated with disabled options and thus result in misinterpretation of meaning. Those with cognitive impairment such as Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) may benefit from high luminance contrast due to their limited attention span, as obscured text may further impair their concentration abilities.

Toggled by the button embedded in the navigation bar, the low luminance contrast mode enables dyslexic individuals to read more efficiently, due to reduced variation in hues, saturation, and brightness. However, achieving this outcome does not come at the expense of functionality, visual aesthetics, and uniformity. This was due to a curated color palette.

Nuanced animations to account for users with photosensitive epilepsy or those susceptible to seizures

In consideration of users with photosensitive epilepsy or those susceptible to seizures, hypermedia was kept to a minimum and animations with flashing content and repetitive rates or patterns were used sparingly. The main animated effects that were used consisted of fade-in and slide-in effects, along with a smooth scroll-to-top effect. Subtle grow-and-shrink animations for the double left caret symbols indicate a text overlay on important images as well. The smooth nature of the fade-in and slide-in animations that occur once does not detract from the main content of the page, thereby satisfying the ‘Three Flashes or Below Threshold’. The use of embedded videos was also avoided as they may slow down the browser’s rendering and reduce perceived responsiveness, and may contain flashing or blinking content. In addition, excessive movement or repetition prevents screen readers from capturing the movement or registering the content accurately.

Usability

Using the Gutenberg Diagram on reading gravity to model the layout of critical information

The layout of the ‘About Us’ page was modelled with the Gutenberg Diagram in mind, which describes a user’s reading gravity (a Z-pattern) and consists of four quadrants – the primary optical area, strong fallow area, weak follow area, and terminal area.

Critical information is presented in the primary optical area, which is the user’s default primary focus and smooths user navigation by limiting lateral movement. The headers and subheaders are strategically positioned in this quadrant to emphasize their significance, as may be seen in the ‘Small Beginnings’ and ‘Our Vision’ sections on the 'About Us' page. The strong fallow area and weak fallow area – the blind spot – are much less regarded, as the supporting images on the 'About Us' page are secondary to the main content and are displayed in these regions.

The terminal area typically causes a pause in the user’s page scan area, which signifies the end of a major section and facilitates the transition into a new section. On the ‘About Us’ page, the terminal area finishes with another block of information that segues either into a section divider (in the case of the first Gutenberg Diagram), or into a completely unique, visually-driven grid layout (as seen with the second Gutenberg Diagram).


Using Fitt’s Law to improve call to action accessibility by limiting pointer movement and increasing the size of clickable fields

According to Fitt’s Law, the ‘time required to move to a target area is a function between the distance to the target and the size of the target’ [36]. Fitt’s Law was integrated into the website by increasing the padding of call to action buttons and reducing pointer movement by making related buttons or links proximal to one another. As a result, precise gestures are not required by the user and the buttons remain accessible even on smaller screen sizes or touch screens, which require more finger dexterity.

To further limit pointer movement, a sticky navigation bar was implemented to render navigation accessible at any point along the vertical scroll bar. As the sticky navigation bar initially limited the viewport, the page title was rendered invisible to correct this error. The inverse of Fitt’s Law was also applied to less pertinent, tangential sidebar information, as the target clicking space was reduced to prevent accidental activation.


Introducing a responsive layout and a toggleable hamburger menu to accommodate smaller screen sizes

Given that the majority of traffic now stems from mobile users, responsive behaviour is critical in smaller screen sizes. A key element in mobile or tablet devices is the introduction of a hamburger menu, which strategically organizes links into a single column and improves overall clickability. The hamburger menu provides clean, compact navigation that increases the main content viewport region on small-sized screens. Without responsive design, the columns would collide or show fewer words per line, thus impairing readability (i.e. by vertically stacking words).

On the website, the sidebars were either moved into a single column to mirror the single column flow of information primed by the toggleable navigation menu, or removed altogether to minimize clutter. Ems and rems were purposefully selected for the font size in order to inherit from parent elements or browser font settings, as pixels are a fixed sized unit that do not adapt to changes in screen dimensions – a problem that is exacerbated by the presence of modern widescreen displays and TVs in addition to mobile devices.

In general, the website was designed to accommodate smaller screen sizes by maintaining layout flexibility, ensuring a clear interface, and creating a focal point by centralizing key blocks of text.

Learning

Learning how to preserve user settings on a page after ending a browser session

According to the Web API section of the MDN web docs, sessionStorage is cleared when the session ends, while localStorage remains accessible since it has no expiration date – with the exception of private browsing. In order to store the user’s settings, a key value pair indicating the current status of the Storage object was stored and its status on page load was monitored to restore its settings. This was implemented in the ‘Toggle Contrast’ button embedded in the navigation menu, in which either the high or low contrast user preferences were recorded.

Since the Storage object is stored purely on the client, it reduces content requests from the web server, which corresponds with faster load times and increased responsiveness. However, there is little control over locally stored data due to a lack of database. With localStorage, developers may potentially lose all information after a user clears their cache and has no method of updating the information after storage.

Determining the use cases of CSS flexbox and CSS grid in relation to their complexity and layout advantages

Through trial and error, I discovered that flex-wrap was not in fact a viable solution to explicit responsive design. The wrapping of content was often not visually sound depending on the context and relationship between the content sections. Flex-wrap was effective when the content was uniform and did not require certain content to be adjacent to other related content. In cases in which proportionality was irrelevant, having five equal columns wrapping into three smaller columns and two larger columns was acceptable.

Visualization of the layout was intuitive with the use of CSS grid, since fractional units could not only be used for both the row tracks and column tracks, but also specify the exact dimensions of a particular element. With flexbox, I realized that one-dimensional layouts provided much more flexibility due to the complete control on alignment (i.e. vertically centering an element) and the expansion of the flexbox element to occupy space via flex-grow. Flexbox had many complex relationships between the containers and the flex properties of its child elements, but was not limited by the left and right positioning limitations of float and the reduced alignment and space distribution control of CSS grid. However, I preferred the grid layout for two-dimensional layouts that required strict control for row and column properties.

Using the jQuery library in order to enable the accessibility button to be toggled and apply the corresponding accessibility options

Throughout this project, I recognized the value in using the lightweight jQuery library as a means of simplifying the DOM tree traversal and manipulating the elements within it. Since jQuery is open source, there were a myriad of tutorials to guide me through the challenging syntax. Moreover, jQuery seamlessly integrated with the Visual Studio IDE through the Manage NuGET Packages dialog box which streamlined access to jQuery functions within the IDE. Learning jQuery facilitated the creation of event handlers and made toggling CSS classes much easier than with standard vanilla JavaScript.

However, one major simplification I noticed was that the syntax required the document to fully load before the JavaScript execution. Having gained invaluable experience working with jQuery, I would continue using vanilla JavaScript for simple front-end tasks, but resort to jQuery for more complex tasks in order to increase my productivity, avoid rewriting existing code, and make use of abstracted, higher-level functions. Given that there are many recurring user-interface widgets in modern websites, I would also make use of jQuery's UI components. In the future, I would make extensive use of jQuery if page load times were important, since jQuery provides the functionality of loading critical information first and loading division elements on an as-needed basis.

Successful Aspects

Use of whitespace to create harmony between sections and deliver a clear message

The use of white space is particularly important in a website that contains both newspaper layouts, which tend to be cluttered, and contact pages, which tend to be cleaner and visually driven. Striking a balance between white space and the preservation of an appropriate design was used to present a navigable and user-friendly interface. The whitespace considerations used in the ‘Contact Us’ and ‘About Us’ pages provided structural harmony to the site by offsetting the more crowded arrangement in the ‘Home’ and ‘Current News’ pages in an effort to send a coherent, comprehensible message. The whitespace was not only used as a separator to delineate different content sections, but also acted as a medium to draw focus to CTA buttons (i.e. increasing whitespace around the CTA as opposed to increasing the padding of the CTA) by creating flow between the elements.

Positioning critical content above the fold to attract user attention on page load

On each page, the section that captures the essence of the entire page is positioned as the first visible element. When a user first loads a page, their engagement with the site is determined by their initial impression.

Every page has a unique identifying ‘above the fold’ layout, each with their own compelling H1 header and visual media. It was crucial to be selective with the content above the fold, so as not to clutter the viewport and thus reduce the bounce rate. The typography is limited to two fonts and the main CTA buttons are subtle – stylistic choices that prioritized simplicity, in order to create cognitive fluency that would ultimately enable the user to process a few options at a time without being subject to an overload of information. Content placement can be further optimised by checking the website analytics to determine the most common screen size that visitors are using and thus more locate the fold with a higher degree of precision.

Areas of Improvement

Incorporate device-independent event handlers to accommodate assistive technologies

With respect to areas of improvement, the incorporation of ‘onfocus’ and ‘onblur’ event handlers for the contact form and CTA buttons would make them device-independent. ‘Onfocus’ may be triggered by a keyboard, a mouse touch, or other assistive technology, which would render the site accessible for non-pointer devices, such as screen readers being used by vision-impaired users. It is essential to validate keyboard accessibility and ensure that the focus order of the page elements coincides with the visual order of the page, so that the focus is never trapped in any one element and cannot be escaped by the keyboard alone. In addition, users should have the option of skipping repetitive blocks of content, such as navigation links and header content, through a skip functionality. It is critical to ensure that accessibility problems do not arise as a byproduct of the event handlers, which may occur when the default behavior of the web browser is modified or the keyboard navigation is obstructed.

Striking a balance between a layout that reflects the semantics of each page name and a site-wide uniformity in page structure

Another obstacle I encountered throughout this project was striking a balance between page layouts that reflected the semantics of the page name and the overall uniformity of structure from page to page. This challenge occasionally compromised the consistent positioning of headers, which led to some discrepancies in font size and decorative elements – notably above the fold.

Moreover, the colour scheme of the website was not always cohesive as it did not complement the colours of the embedded images. A potential solution could be to use a colour palette that clashes less with the images, or to construct a custom colour palette based on the average pixel colour of the embedded images. An incohesive colour scheme could pose problems in an auto-updated site or one that uses a single template and has its content frequently updated by new items from a database. In order to combat this issue, a function could be written to generate and compute the ideal colour scheme for the current set of images. However, this solution could potentially diminish the user experience if a user is already accustomed to a previous colour scheme.
